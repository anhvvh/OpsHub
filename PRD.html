Product Requirements · Plain-language edition

OpsHub

An AI-powered operations cockpit that lets a small online shop see its whole business — orders, stock, payments, and warnings — on one calm screen.

Version 1.0 Type Portfolio MVP Build budget ~2 days, one person + AI Currency Swedish krona (SEK)

01 The idea, in plain terms

The problem we're solving and the shape of the answer.

The problem

A small online shop is run across five or six separate tools — the web store, a marketplace like Amazon, a payment service, an inventory list, and a spreadsheet. None of them talks to the others. So the owner has no single place that answers the one question they ask every morning: "How is my business doing right now, and what needs my attention?"

Because nobody is watching automatically, problems are found late — a bestseller sells out mid-promotion, a payment provider quietly starts failing, refunds spike on a bad batch — usually only after a customer complains or the payout comes in smaller than expected. Industry research suggests juggling these tools can cost a multi-channel shop up to 14 hours a week.

Why the tools they already have don't fix it

  • Big business systems (ERPs) take months and tens of thousands of kronor to set up — built for accounting depth, not a quick daily glance.
  • Store and marketplace dashboards each only show their own corner. Two sales channels still means two separate pictures to reconcile by hand.
  • Payment dashboards are excellent at payments but blind to stock and orders.
  • Spreadsheets are fully manual and go out of date the moment someone forgets to update them.

The solution

Meet OpsHub — a lightweight web dashboard that brings the four things a merchant checks daily (orders, stock, payments, revenue) into one clean view, and adds an AI Operations Assistant that taps the owner on the shoulder about the few things that actually need attention — instead of waiting to be asked.

What OpsHub is not

It is not a cash register (POS), not a giant business system (ERP), and not a store builder. It's a calm monitoring cockpit with just a couple of real actions (update an order, adjust stock). The goal: understand your business in under two minutes each morning.

This is a portfolio project

OpsHub exists to demonstrate product, business-analysis, and system-design thinking — not to be sold. Success means a reviewer grasps the problem, the thinking, and the value within about two minutes of opening the app, and the result feels polished and credible rather than a rough prototype.

02 Who it's for

Two realistic people the product is designed around.

Primary user

Maria — the owner

Her business
Founder and only full-time employee of a Swedish skincare brand. About 40–80 orders a day, ~150 products, selling through her own store plus Amazon.
What she wants
To catch problems before customers or her accountant do — and spend less time "checking things" across five browser tabs every morning.
Comfort with tech
Fluent with everyday apps, but not a developer. Wants things ready-to-read at a glance, not reports she has to build.

Secondary user

Jonas — the assistant

His role
Part-time helper at a slightly larger shop (5–10 people). Handles daily order triage and customer replies.
What he wants
A simple, guided view of what needs attention today — without admin access to every backend tool.
Comfort with tech
Confident with email and spreadsheets; not a power user of complex business software.

03 What you can do

The five parts of OpsHub, each written as the goals it serves. Click any part to expand its user stories.

Part 1

Merchant Dashboard — the home screen

›

One glance answers "how is today going, and what needs me?" This is the centerpiece.

  • As a merchant, I want today's revenue, order count, pending payments, and open refunds shown as big clear numbers, so I know how the day is going without opening any other tool.
  • As a merchant, I want each number shown next to a comparison with a normal period, so I can tell whether today is unusual.
  • As a merchant, I want low-stock warnings right on the home screen, so I never sell out of a bestseller by surprise.
  • As a merchant, I want the AI insights shown prominently, so the most important issues find me rather than me hunting for them.
  • As a merchant, I want every number and warning to be clickable straight to its detail screen, so I go from "something's off" to "here's why" in one click.
Part 2

Orders

›

See the order pipeline and keep it accurate.

  • As a merchant, I want a list of all orders with clear status badges, so I can see the state of my pipeline at a glance.
  • As a merchant, I want to filter orders by status (Pending payment, Paid, Fulfilled, Delivered, Refunded, Cancelled), so I can work one queue at a time.
  • As a merchant, I want an order detail page with items, customer, amounts, and payment state together, so I don't check three tools.
  • As a merchant, I want a timeline of what happened on each order, so I can reconstruct the story quickly.
  • As a merchant, I want to update an order's status and have it stick, so the pipeline stays true when something happens off-system — with the change recorded in the timeline.
Part 3

Inventory

›

Know stock health without a warehouse tool.

  • As a merchant, I want a product list with current stock levels, so I can review stock health in one place.
  • As a merchant, I want products below their low-stock line flagged visually, so at-risk items stand out instantly.
  • As a merchant, I want a product detail page (price, code, stock, threshold, recent sales), so I can decide whether to reorder.
  • As a merchant, I want to adjust a product's stock count and have it stick, so I can correct the number after a physical count — and the low-stock warning re-checks immediately.
Part 4

Payments

›

Payment health, visible without opening Stripe or Klarna.

  • As a merchant, I want a payments list with statuses (pending, succeeded, failed, refunded), so payment health is visible in one place.
  • As a merchant, I want failed payments called out separately, so I catch processor trouble within hours, not days.
  • As a merchant, I want to see which refunds are still pending, so I know which customers are waiting for money back.
  • As a merchant, I want a daily payment summary, so end-of-day reconciliation takes seconds.
  • As a merchant, I want each payment linked to its order, so I can jump from a failed payment to the affected order in one click.
Part 5

AI Operations Assistant

›

Not a chatbot — a short, ranked list of "here's what needs you, and what to do." Detailed in section 5.

  • As a merchant, I want to be warned proactively about low stock, rising payment failures, falling revenue, a surprise bestseller, or a refund spike — so problems find me early.
  • As a merchant, I want each insight as a short, number-first headline plus one brief action, so I grasp it at a glance and know what to do — not a paragraph to read.
  • As a merchant, I want insights ranked and kept few, so the assistant never becomes noise I learn to ignore.
  • As a merchant, I want each insight to deep-link to the relevant product, order, or payment, so acting on it takes one click.

04 The screens

Six screens in total. The AI Assistant lives on the dashboard rather than on its own screen — keeping warnings on the home view is the whole point.

1

Merchant Dashboard /

Headline numbers (revenue, orders, pending payments, open refunds) with comparisons, the low-stock list, and the AI insights panel — everything clickable.

2

Orders list /orders

A table of orders — number, date, customer, channel, total, and status badge — with filter tabs, newest first.

3

Order detail /orders/…

Customer, items, totals, linked payment state, an event timeline, and the control to update the order's status.

4

Inventory list /inventory

Products with code, price, current stock, and threshold — low-stock rows visually distinct.

5

Product detail /inventory/…

Product facts, stock versus threshold, a recent-sales snapshot, and the control to adjust the stock count.

6

Payments /payments

A daily summary card, the payments table with status badges and failure reasons, failed payments highlighted, each row linking to its order.

05 The AI Assistant, in detail

Five kinds of warning. Simple rules decide when to raise one and work out the numbers; the AI phrases them as a short headline plus a brief suggested action.

Each insight is number-first and glanceable — not a paragraph. A fixed rulebook watches the data, decides something is worth flagging, and the AI turns the numbers into a crisp headline with one short action line, for example:

Refunds +40% — 6 of 9 on Lavender Face Serum
→ Check the latest batch.

If the AI is ever unavailable, a plain template built from the same numbers is shown instead, so the demo never breaks.

Stock running low

Fixed threshold

Lavender Face Serum: 4 left (low at 15). → Reorder now.

Payment failures rising

Fixed threshold

7 failed payments in 24h (usual: 1–2). → Check the payment provider.

Revenue trending down

Vs. prior period

Revenue −20% vs. last week. → Review before month-end.

A surprise bestseller

Vs. prior period

Nordic Night Cream: sales 3× last week. → Protect stock, push marketing.

Refund spike

Vs. prior period

Refunds +40% — 6 of 9 on Lavender Serum. → Check the latest batch.

A deliberate design choice

There is no stored "alerts" list. The AI insights are worked out fresh from the current data every time the dashboard loads — so you "clear" a warning simply by fixing what caused it, and it disappears on its own. Less to maintain, and it can never show a stale warning.

Why this matters

The sample data is treated as a real part of the product, not an afterthought. The demo's credibility rests on it telling a believable month in the life of Hälsa Skincare — with five genuine problems waiting to be found.

08 How we'll know it works

What "finished and correct" means for this project.

The success bar

A reviewer opening the live app or the README should, within about two minutes, understand the problem, see the product thinking, and feel that the execution is polished and credible.

What gets automatically tested

  • The warning rules — the heart of the app. Each of the five rules is checked with a case that should fire, a case that shouldn't, and a borderline case exactly on the threshold. Rules that compare to a previous period are also checked for "no earlier data" so they can't misfire.
  • The two saving actions — updating an order's status saves and adds the right timeline entry; adjusting stock saves and immediately re-checks the low-stock warning. Invalid changes are rejected.

What's checked by hand instead

Visual polish and the exact wording of the AI (which naturally varies) are reviewed manually against a short demo checklist: open the dashboard → see the five insights → click each through to its detail screen → update one order → adjust one stock level → watch the low-stock warning disappear.

09 What's deliberately left out

Saying no is the point — every competitor studied got into trouble by adding depth and clutter. These are out of scope on purpose.

  • Live connections to real Shopify, Stripe, Klarna, or Amazon accounts
  • Logins, user accounts, roles, or multiple businesses
  • A chat/conversation interface for the assistant
  • Full-text order search, bulk actions, merging channels
  • Multi-warehouse stock syncing or batch/serial tracking
  • Demand forecasting
  • Payment disputes, bank reconciliation, payout scheduling
  • Accounting or bookkeeping features
  • Customer-service / helpdesk tools; shipping labels
  • Email, SMS, or Slack notifications
  • Native mobile apps (the web app is responsive; desktop is the demo target)

10 Build & delivery notes

The practical decisions, kept brief.

DecisionChoice
How it's builtA single Next.js web app (React + TypeScript). Secrets like the AI key stay on the server, never in the browser.
Where data livesA hosted Postgres database, so the two "save" actions survive a page reload.
Where the data comes fromSeeded sample data only — no live account connections (built so a real one could be added later).
Sign-inNone — it opens straight onto the dashboard (single demo shop).
Look & feelWarm, editorial, minimal — calm and legible, deliberately unlike cluttered enterprise tools.
Where it runsA live web address (Vercel), plus a public code repository.
What shipsThe live app, a repository with a README, a short written case study, and a brief demo video/GIF.
